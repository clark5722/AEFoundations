Risk

Human beings have provided some form of insurance to one another for at least as long as recorded time (e.g., Bogucki (2011)), and the modern world is full of institutional arrangements that protect individuals against risk. One way way to derive demand for these institutions from an economic model is to assume that individuals are inherently risk averse in the sense that they are willing to pay something now in return for a more certain outcome in the future. The expected utility model is a simple representation for this sort of preference. In it, we assume that individuals can rank choices that influence the probability distribution over uncertain future outcomes by comparing the expected value of an increasing state-independent concave utility function \(u(\cdot)\) across choices.
